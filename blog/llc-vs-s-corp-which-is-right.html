LLC vs S-Corp: Which Is Right for Your Business?

Understand the tax differences, the $60K rule, and when each structure makes sense

If you're starting or growing a business, you've probably heard that an S-Corp election can save you thousands in taxes. But is an LLC vs S-Corp really that simple? The answer depends on your profits, your goals, and how much complexity you're willing to manage. This guide breaks down the key differences between an LLC and an S-Corp in 2026, including when to stick with a standard LLC and when the S-Corp election starts saving you real money.

First: LLC and S-Corp Aren't the Same Thing

This is the number one point of confusion. An LLC is a business entity — it's a legal structure you register with your state. An S-Corp is a tax classification — it's how the IRS taxes your business. You can have an LLC that's taxed as an S-Corp. They're not mutually exclusive; they operate on different levels.

Here's how it works in practice:

  • Default LLC: Profits pass through to your personal tax return. You pay income tax and self-employment tax (15.3%) on all profits.
  • LLC with S-Corp election: You pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll taxes), and remaining profits are distributed as dividends — not subject to self-employment tax.

The $60K Profit Threshold: When S-Corp Starts Making Sense

The magic number most CPAs reference is $60,000 in net profit. Here's why:

With a standard LLC earning $100,000 in profit, you'd owe about $15,300 in self-employment tax (15.3%) on top of income tax. With an S-Corp election, you might pay yourself a $50,000 salary (paying ~$7,650 in payroll taxes) and take the remaining $50,000 as a distribution — saving roughly $7,650 per year.

But if your profits are only $30,000? The savings shrink dramatically — and the extra accounting costs ($1,000–$2,000/year for payroll services and S-Corp tax returns) can eat up or exceed those savings.

✅ General rule: If your LLC nets less than ~$60,000/year in profit, stick with the standard LLC. Above $60K, talk to a CPA about the S-Corp election. Above $100K, the S-Corp almost always saves money.

When to Stay as a Standard LLC

  • You're just starting out and profits are under $40K–$60K.
  • You want simplicity. No payroll, simpler tax filing, less bookkeeping.
  • You're a side hustle or freelancer with variable income.
  • You plan to reinvest most profits back into the business.
  • You have foreign owners or multiple classes of membership — S-Corps don't allow this.

When to Elect S-Corp Status

  • Net profits consistently exceed $60,000/year.
  • You can pay yourself a reasonable salary and still have significant distributions left over.
  • You're comfortable with payroll (or willing to pay for a payroll service).
  • You're a single-member or small multi-member LLC with only U.S. citizen/resident members.
  • You have a good CPA who can handle the 1120-S filing.

How to Make the S-Corp Election

If you decide S-Corp taxation is right for you, here's the process:

  1. Form your LLC in your state — check our state-by-state guides for instructions.
  2. File IRS Form 2553 within 75 days of forming your LLC (or by March 15 for the current tax year).
  3. Set up payroll and begin paying yourself a reasonable salary.
  4. File Form 1120-S annually, issuing K-1s to all members.
⚠️ "Reasonable salary" matters. The IRS scrutinizes S-Corp owners who pay themselves too little to avoid payroll taxes. Your salary should match what someone in your role would earn in the open market. Underpaying yourself is a red flag for audits.

Real Example: $120,000 in Profit

Standard LLC

  • Self-employment tax: $120,000 × 15.3% = $18,360
  • Accounting costs: ~$500
  • Total tax-related cost: ~$18,860

S-Corp (with $55,000 salary)

  • Payroll tax on salary: $55,000 × 15.3% = $8,415
  • Distribution (no SE tax): $65,000 × 0% = $0
  • Accounting/payroll costs: ~$2,000
  • Total tax-related cost: ~$10,415
💰 Annual savings with S-Corp: ~$8,445. Over 5 years, that's more than $42,000 in your pocket instead of going to self-employment tax.

S-Corp Restrictions to Know

  • Maximum of 100 shareholders
  • All shareholders must be U.S. citizens or permanent residents
  • Only one class of stock (though voting/non-voting is allowed)
  • Certain entities (other corporations, partnerships) cannot be shareholders
  • Must use a calendar year-end (with few exceptions)

Bottom Line: LLC vs S-Corp in 2026

For most new business owners, start with a standard LLC. It's simpler, cheaper to maintain, and gives you full liability protection. As your profits grow past the $60,000 mark, revisit the S-Corp election with a qualified CPA.

The beauty of an LLC is flexibility — you can always elect S-Corp status later without changing your legal entity. Start lean, grow smart, and optimize your taxes when the numbers justify it.
